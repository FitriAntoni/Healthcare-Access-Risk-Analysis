# Healthcare Access Risk Analysis (Interactive Dashboard in Microsoft Excel)

## Project Overview

This project analyzes healthcare access and community health indicators from **Care Bridge Health Network** to identify counties at the highest health risk. The dashboard highlights disparities in insurance coverage, access to primary care providers, and health outcomes, enabling healthcare organizations to prioritize interventions and allocate resources more effectively.

---

## Project Objective

- Identify counties with the highest healthcare access risk.
- Evaluate disparities in insurance coverage and access to primary care providers.
- Analyze the relationship between healthcare accessibility and community health outcomes.
- Support data-driven decisions for resource allocation, preventive care, and healthcare planning.

## Key Analytical Questions

- Which counties face the highest healthcare access risk?
- Where are uninsured rates most severe?
- Which counties have the weakest access to primary care providers?
- Which counties show the poorest health outcomes across multiple indicators?
- Which states have the highest concentration of high-risk counties?
- Do counties with weaker provider access also experience poorer health outcomes?
- Which counties should Care Bridge prioritize for mobile clinics, insurance outreach, and preventive care programs?

## Key Insights

- **Kennedy County** was identified as the highest-risk county, recording consistently high levels across multiple health indicators, including uninsured rate, poor mental health, poor physical health, and overall poor health outcomes.
- Although **Milan County** had the weakest access to primary care providers, it was not among the counties with the poorest physical health outcomes.
- The five states with the highest concentration of high-risk counties were **Alaska, Alabama, Arkansas, Iowa, and Missouri**.
- Limited access to primary care providers did not consistently correspond to poorer health outcomes. For example, Milan County experienced limited provider access but relatively better health outcomes, whereas Kennedy County had comparatively better provider access yet ranked among the highest-risk counties.

---

## Conclusion

The analysis demonstrates that community health outcomes are influenced by multiple interconnected factors rather than primary care access alone. While provider availability remains important, counties with relatively adequate access may still experience poor health outcomes due to broader socioeconomic and public health challenges. A comprehensive evaluation of multiple health indicators is essential for accurately identifying high-risk communities and prioritizing healthcare resources.

---

## Recommendations

- Prioritize interventions in high-risk counties, particularly **Kennedy County**, by addressing insurance coverage, mental health, physical health, and overall community well-being. The findings can guide decisions on **deploying mobile clinics, expanding insurance outreach, strengthening preventive care programs, and prioritizing primary care provider expansion**.
- Conduct further analysis to identify socioeconomic and environmental factors contributing to poor health outcomes beyond healthcare accessibility.
- Allocate healthcare resources using multiple health risk indicators instead of relying solely on primary care provider availability.
- Investigate why counties such as **Milan County** maintain relatively positive health outcomes despite limited provider access and assess whether these practices can be replicated elsewhere.
- Focus public health planning and resource allocation on **Alaska, Alabama, Arkansas, Iowa, and Missouri**, where high-risk counties are more concentrated.
